[4] Isentropic hybrid stars in the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model: effects of neutrino trapping

  • Authors: Andrea Sabatucci, Armen Sedrakian
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19085v1
  • Methodology: Simulation (Equation of State modeling with mixed-phase prescription)
  • Core Results: Neutrino trapping in hot, lepton-rich proto-neutron stars significantly modifies particle composition and shifts the onset of quark matter deconfinement to higher densities. Hot, neutrino-rich hybrid star configurations have larger radii and slightly higher maximum masses than their cold counterparts, with radii increasing by several kilometers.

[5] G objects as Primordial Black Hole-Neutron Star Remnants: Population Modeling and Multi-Wavelength Observables

  • Authors: David Morales-Zapien, Stefano Profumo
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19083v1
  • Methodology: Theory (Population modeling of compact object capture)
  • Core Results: Proposes that the enigmatic G objects orbiting the Galactic Center are neutron stars converted into low-mass black holes via capture of primordial black holes, a process that also explains the deficit of ordinary pulsars in this region. The model predicts a suite of testable multi-wavelength signatures across infrared, radio, X-ray, and microlensing channels.

[6] GRB 240205B: A Reverse Shock Detected in Rapid Response Radio Observations

  • Authors: S. I. Chastain, G. E. Anderson, A. J. van der Horst, L. Rhodes, C. Morley, A. Gulati, J. K. Leung, T. D. Russel, S. D. Ryder
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19047v1
  • Methodology: Observation (Broadband radio modeling)
    • Energy band: Radio
    • Key Targets/Sample: GRB 240205B
  • Core Results: Rapid-response ATCA observations beginning 13 minutes post-burst detected the earliest GRB radio afterglow at ~35 minutes. The early-time radio light curve reveals a clear reverse shock component, which is best modeled alongside a forward shock in a stellar wind medium. The data constrain a model-independent minimum bulk Lorentz factor Γmin ≈ 100 at 35 minutes.

[12] Spin-up and spin distribution of stellar black holes grown by gas accretion in proto-stellar clusters

  • Authors: Zacharias Roupas
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18857v1
  • Methodology: Theory (Modeling of chaotic accretion and spin evolution)
  • Core Results: Predicts a strong spin-mass correlation for stellar black holes grown via chaotic accretion in compact proto-stellar clusters. Low-spin BHs (a* ≤ 0.3) are predominantly low-mass (mBH ≲ 25 M☉), while high-spin BHs (a* ≥ 0.7) are predominantly high-mass (mBH ≳ 65 M☉). The median spin for mBH ≥ 100 M☉ is ā* ~ 0.90, consistent with the estimated spin of the gravitational-wave event GW231123.

[13] UGC 2369S: a Kpc Scale Triple Merger Candidate Identified in a Nearby Luminous Infrared Galaxy

  • Authors: Yuanze Ding, Michael J. Koss, Fiona A. Harrison ★(Famous Scholar), Charles C. Steidel, Connor Auge, Jared Gillette, Erica Hammerstein, Ruancun Li, Macon Magno, Ignacio del Moral-Castro, Alessandro Peca, Claudio Ricci, Yiqing Song, Ezequiel Treister, Zhuyun Zhuang
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18499v1
  • Methodology: Observation (Multi-wavelength analysis with AO, Chandra, radio)
    • Energy band: Optical/NIR, X-ray, Radio
    • Key Targets/Sample: UGC 2369S
  • Core Results: Identifies UGC 2369S as a clear triple-nucleus merger with ~3 kpc separations. The northern core shows evidence for a heavily obscured AGN with Av > 5 and NH ≳ 1025 cm-2, while the other two cores exhibit tidal disruption signatures. The system's strongly varying gravitational potential may have cannibalized the stellar bulge of one core.

[14] A systematic search for physical associations between fast radio bursts and astrophysical transients

  • Authors: Hao-Hao Chen, Wen-Tao Xu, Xin-Yu Liang, Ming-Xuan Lu, Can-Min Deng
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18487v1
  • Methodology: Methods (3D Bayesian inference for spatial cross-matching)
  • Core Results: Recovers the known high-significance association between FRB 20180916B and AT 2020hur with a probability of 0.9998. Finds no new statistically significant FRB-AT associations among 3765 FRBs and candidate optical/GRB transients, demonstrating that small angular separation alone is insufficient and high-precision FRB localization is essential for definitive identification.

[16] Known changing-look AGN located within Rubin Deep Drilling Fields

  • Authors: Mariangella Camus, Swayamtrupta Panda
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18255v1
  • Methodology: Methods (Catalog cross-matching with survey footprints)
  • Core Results: Identifies 79 known changing-look AGN within the Rubin Observatory's Wide-Fast-Deep survey footprint, including 5 particularly favorable targets located in the high-cadence Deep Drilling Fields (DDFs) of COSMOS and XMM-LSS. This provides a proof of concept for connecting known variable AGN with future Rubin era variability studies.

[17] Gamma-Ray Bursts as an Independent High-Redshift Probe of Dark Energy

  • Authors: Maria Giovanna Dainotti, Aleksander Łukasz Lenart, Biagio De Simone, William Giarè, Eleonora Di Valentino, Dieter H. Hartmann, Nissim Fraija, Kazunari Iwasaki, Gaetano Lambiase
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18223v1
  • Methodology: Methods (Forecast based on GRB luminosity relations)
  • Core Results: Forecasts show that a sample of ~66 optical GRBs with plateau phases, calibrated via the Dainotti relation, can achieve a constraint on the dark energy equation-of-state parameter of σw ≈ 0.47, comparable to Planck CMB results within the wCDM model. GRBs can thus provide an independent, high-redshift cosmological probe to test dynamical dark energy.

[18] Neutrinos and gamma rays from Seyfert galaxies constrain the properties of coronal turbulence

  • Authors: Federico Testagrossa, Damiano F. G. Fiorillo, Luca Comisso, Enrico Peretti, Maria Petropoulou, Lorenzo Sironi
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18214v1
  • Methodology: Theory (Joint multimessenger modeling of hadronic emission)
  • Core Results: A joint fit to IceCube neutrino and Fermi-LAT gamma-ray data from NGC 1068 constrains its corona to be compact, strongly magnetized (β ≲ 0.1), and with a large turbulence coherence length relative to its size. For NGC 7469, the data favor a larger corona with much smaller coherence length, high magnetization, but a very small fraction (fp ~ 10-5) of energy in non-thermal protons.

  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18156v1
  • Methodology: Observation (X-ray spectral analysis with XMM-Newton and NuSTAR)
    • Energy band: X-ray (0.3-40 keV rest frame)
    • Key Targets/Sample: 23 quasars from SUBWAYS (0.1bol ~ 2×1044-2×1046 erg/s)
  • Core Results: The hard X-ray spectra reveal a median high-energy cut-off of Ecut = 87 keV and optical depth τ ~ 1-5, indicating a cooler, moderately optically thick corona in luminous quasars compared to Seyferts. [22] High-Frequency Gravitational Waves from Phase Transitions in Nascent Neutron Stars

  • Authors: Katarina Bleau, Joachim Kopp, Jiheon Lee, Jorinde van de Vis
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18153v1
  • Methodology: Theory (Signal prediction from first-order phase transitions)
  • Core Results: Argues that the formation of a quark matter core during a galactic supernova, via a first-order phase transition, could emit gravitational waves with characteristic frequencies in the MHz band and strain amplitudes of h ~ 10-26. This presents a novel target for future high-frequency gravitational wave detectors to probe quantum chromodynamics in neutron star interiors.

[23] Spectral Hardness as the Primary Discriminator: Unveiling the Collapsar--Merger Boundary with a Gold-Standard Gamma-Ray Burst Sample

  • Authors: Xue Zhang, Yan-Kun Qu, Shuang-Xi Yi, Yu-Peng Yang, Fen Lyu, Fa-Yin Wang, Zhong-Xiao Man
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18150v1
  • Methodology: Methods (Support Vector Machine classification)
  • Core Results: Derives a quantitative classifier ISVM = 5.01 log10 Ep,i - 1.25 log10 Eiso - 0.34 log10 T90,z - 12.90, where events with ISVM > 0 are mergers. The analysis reveals spectral hardness (Ep,i) has ~5 times the discriminative power of duration (T90), establishing it as the primary physical signature distinguishing merger from collapsar progenitors.
