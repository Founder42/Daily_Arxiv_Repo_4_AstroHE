[2] Searching for dark matter X-ray lines from the Large Magellanic Cloud with eROSITA

  • Authors: Jorge Terol Calvo, Marco Taoso, Andrea Caputo, Michela Negro, Marco Regis
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19109v1
  • Methodology: Observation (Analysis of eROSITA-DE DR1 datasets)
    • Energy band: X-ray
    • Key Targets/Sample: Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) halo
  • Core Results: Analysis of the 1–9 keV spectrum reveals no evidence for monochromatic X-ray lines from sterile neutrino or ALP decay. New constraints on the active-sterile neutrino mixing angle sin2(2θ) and ALP-photon coupling gaγ are established, significantly strengthening limits for dark matter masses below 5 keV.

[5] G objects as Primordial Black Hole-Neutron Star Remnants: Population Modeling and Multi-Wavelength Observables

  • Authors: David Morales-Zapien, Stefano Profumo ★(Famous Scholar)
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19083v1
  • Methodology: Simulation (Population-level framework and capture physics)
  • Core Results: G-objects orbiting the Galactic Center are modeled as low-mass black hole remnants (M < 2.5 M☉) formed by primordial black hole capture within neutron stars. This scenario simultaneously explains the G-object dynamical stability and the "missing pulsar problem" in the central parsec, predicting specific X-ray and radio signatures distinguishable from stellar models.

[8] GWTC-4.0: Tests of General Relativity. III. Tests of the Remnants

  • Authors: The LIGO Scientific Collaboration, the Virgo Collaboration, KAGRA Collaboration ★(Famous Scholar), et al.
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19021v1
  • Methodology: Observation (Gravitational-wave spectroscopy and ringdown analysis)
    • Energy band: Gravitational Wave
    • Key Targets/Sample: 42 confident events from O4a
  • Core Results: Ringdown analysis of 42 binary mergers confirms that the remnants are consistent with Kerr black holes as predicted by General Relativity. No evidence for post-merger echoes or deviations in the quasinormal mode spectrum was detected, though one hierarchical analysis places the GR prediction at the 98.6+1.4-9.4% credible boundary.

[9] GWTC-4.0: Tests of General Relativity. II. Parameterized Tests

  • Authors: The LIGO Scientific Collaboration, the Virgo Collaboration, KAGRA Collaboration ★(Famous Scholar), et al.
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19020v1
  • Methodology: Observation (Parameterized post-Newtonian analysis)
    • Energy band: Gravitational Wave
    • Key Targets/Sample: GWTC-4.0 catalog
  • Core Results: Bounds on deviations from General Relativity post-Newtonian coefficients are improved by factors of 1.2–5.5 across the sample. The update provides the most stringent limit to date on the graviton mass, constrained to mg ≤ 1.92 × 10-23 eV/c2 at 90% credibility.

[10] GWTC-4.0: Tests of General Relativity. I. Overview and General Tests

  • Authors: The LIGO Scientific Collaboration, the Virgo Collaboration, KAGRA Collaboration ★(Famous Scholar), et al.
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19019v1
  • Methodology: Observation (Multi-detector consistency tests)
    • Energy band: Gravitational Wave
    • Key Targets/Sample: 91 total events including 42 from O4a
  • Core Results: Residual analysis confirms that GW waveforms are consistent with GR predictions within detector noise limits for the entire sample. Inferences of final black hole mass and spin from subdominant multipole moments show no evidence for non-GR polarization modes or modified gravity effects in the strong-field regime.

[11] GRB 241030A: a bright afterglow challenging forward shock emission

  • Authors: J. -G. Ducoin, C. Pellouin, F. Daigne ★(Famous Scholar), N. Omodei ★(Famous Scholar), et al.
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18956v1
  • Methodology: Observation (Multi-wavelength photometric campaign)
    • Energy band: X-ray, Gamma-ray, UV, Optical
    • Key Targets/Sample: GRB 241030A (z = 1.411)
  • Core Results: The bright X-ray and optical afterglow defies standard forward-shock models unless extreme kinetic energies and very low prompt efficiencies are assumed. The required Synchrotron Self-Compton (SSC) contribution suggests a highly magnetized jet environment with low electron energy fractions (εe), hinting at a unique sub-class of energetic transients.

[12] Spin-up and spin distribution of stellar black holes grown by gas accretion in proto-stellar clusters

  • Authors: Zacharias Roupas
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18857v1
  • Methodology: Simulation (Stochastic accretion disk modeling)
  • Core Results: Stochastic gas accretion in compact clusters (z ~ 10) produces a strong spin-mass correlation for stellar black holes within ~10 Myr. High-mass black holes (M > 100 M☉) saturate at median spins a* ~ 0.90, while low-mass counterparts (M < 25 M☉) remain at a* ≤ 0.3, matching recent gravitational wave observations.

[13] UGC 2369S: a Kpc Scale Triple Merger Candidate Identified in a Nearby Luminous Infrared Galaxy

  • Authors: Yuanze Ding, Michael J. Koss ★(Famous Scholar), Fiona A. Harrison, Claudio Ricci ★(Famous Scholar), Ezequiel Treister ★(Famous Scholar), et al.
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18499v1
  • Methodology: Observation (High-res X-ray and NIR adaptive optics)
    • Energy band: X-ray, NIR, Radio, Optical
    • Key Targets/Sample: UGC 2369S triple merger
  • Core Results: Chandra X-ray and HST/AO imaging reveal a triple-nucleus merger at ~3 kpc separations, one of the closest systems ever resolved. The northern core is identified as a candidate heavily obscured AGN with NH ≳ 1025 cm-2 and MBH ≃ 108 M☉, though high dust extinction (Av > 5) masks typical optical/X-ray AGN signatures.

[16] Known changing-look AGN located within Rubin Deep Drilling Fields

  • Authors: Mariangella Camus, Swayamtrupta Panda
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18255v1
  • Methodology: Methods (Catalog cross-matching for time-domain surveys)
  • Core Results: A master catalog of 79 known changing-look AGN has been identified within the Rubin/LSST Wide-Fast-Deep footprint. Five specific targets are located in the high-cadence Deep Drilling Fields (COSMOS and XMM-LSS), providing a high-priority sample for studying rapid transitions in accretion disk and corona states.

[18] Neutrinos and gamma rays from Seyfert galaxies constrain the properties of coronal turbulence

  • Authors: Federico Testagrossa, Damiano F. G. Fiorillo, Luca Comisso, Enrico Peretti, Maria Petropoulou ★(Famous Scholar), Lorenzo Sironi ★(Famous Scholar)
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18214v1
  • Methodology: Theory (Multimessenger joint fitting of IceCube and Fermi-LAT)
  • Core Results: The TeV neutrino signal from NGC 1068 implies a compact, strongly magnetized corona where turbulence coherence lengths are large relative to the source size. Conversely, NGC 7469 data suggest a larger corona with smaller coherence scales and a significantly lower fraction of energy diverted into non-thermal protons.

[21] Supermassive Black Hole Winds in X-rays: SUBWAYS V. Properties of hot coronae in quasars at intermediate redshift

  • Authors: S. Peluso, G. Lanzuisi, A. Comastri ★(Famous Scholar), M. Brusa ★(Famous Scholar), M. Giustini ★(Famous Scholar), G. Miniutti ★(Famous Scholar), S. Bianchi ★(Famous Scholar), G. Ponti ★(Famous Scholar), et al.
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18156v1
  • Methodology: Observation (XMM-Newton and NuSTAR spectroscopy)
    • Energy band: X-ray (0.3–40 keV rest-frame)
    • Key Targets/Sample: 23 intermediate-redshift quasars (0.1 < z < 0.4)
  • Core Results: High-resolution X-ray spectroscopy reveals a significant anticorrelation between the high-energy cut-off (Ecut) and both Eddington ratio (λEdd) and bolometric luminosity (Lbol). Luminous quasars exhibit cooler (Ecut ~ 90–100 keV) and optically thicker (τ ~ 1–5) hot coronae compared to low-luminosity Seyfert galaxies, indicating enhanced coronal cooling likely driven by pair-production processes.

[23] Spectral Hardness as the Primary Discriminator: Unveiling the Collapsar--Merger Boundary with a Gold-Standard Gamma-Ray Burst Sample

  • Authors: Xue Zhang, Yan-Kun Qu, Shuang-Xi Yi, Yu-Peng Yang, Fen Lyu, Fa-Yin Wang ★(Famous Scholar), Zhong-Xiao Man
  • Link: http://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18150v1
  • Methodology: Methods (Support Vector Machine classification)
  • Core Results: A new SVM-based classification index (ISVM) shows that spectral hardness (Ep,i) is five times more effective than duration (T90,z) for distinguishing black hole mergers from stellar collapsars. The boundary defines a strict hardness ceiling for collapsars, identifying mergers as energetic outliers, which improves progenitor identification in high-redshift X-ray and Gamma-ray samples.
